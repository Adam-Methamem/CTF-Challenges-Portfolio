The Nine Days of Odin
In the age before memory, before rune or rhyme,
When nine great realms hung suspended in time —
Asgard above, where the golden halls gleam,
Midgard below, caught between waking and dream,
Jotunheim shaking with frost-giant breath,
Helheim below wrapped in stillness and death,
Niflheim frozen in mist cold as bone,
Muspelheim burning on embers alone,
Vanaheim dreaming in meadows of gold,
Alfheim where light-elves sing legends of old,
Svartalfheim carved in the deep dark below —
Of all who walked these nine realms, one alone chose to go.

Not for glory. Not in battle. Not by another's hand.
The Allfather stepped forward, alone, as planned.
He found Yggdrasil — the great ash, the spine of all that is —
And the wind said nothing. The worlds held their breath. This moment was his.
He climbed no steps. He called no name.
He cast himself upon the bough — and the tree received him like a flame.

One — the first day — he hung, and the sky didn't weep.
The ravens watched. The roots went deep.
No god reached up. No god reached down.
Only silence, and wind, and the turning of worlds around.
Two — the second day — his body grew cold,
But his eye burned on — that single eye, bold,
The other long traded for a sip from the well.
He had already paid. He knew how to dwell in the fell.
Three — the third day — the frost found his skin,
Yet something below had started to stir within.
The runes — old and older than old —
Shifted like embers beneath the world's fold.
Four — the fourth day — he cried to the void,
No answer was given, no comfort deployed.
Even a god, when he truly seeks,
Must starve in the silence for days and for weeks.
Five — the fifth day — the tree began to hum,
A low ancient trembling that left the tongue numb.
Yggdrasil remembered the things carved in bark,
And Odin pressed deeper into the dark.
Six — the sixth day — his breath became thin,
A door at the edge of all being swung in.
He could see them — the shapes — the cut lines of things,
The letters that carry the weight that fate brings.
Seven — the seventh day — they rose like a tide,
The runes swimming upward with nowhere to hide.
He reached — though his arms were bound fast to the tree —
He reached with his will, and the runes let him see.
Eight — the eighth day — he wept and he burned,
For wisdom is never just given — it's earned.
Each symbol a wound. Each letter a scar.
Each rune was a world, and each world was a war.
Nine — the ninth day — he screamed and he took them —
Grabbed the runes whole, let their fire run through him.

The tree shook. The serpent looked up from below.
The eagle cried out from the crown. The winds didn't know
what to do with a god who had died and still breathed,
who had given himself to the thing he believed —
not in power or throne or the praise of the brave,
but in knowledge — the only thing worth the grave.

He fell from Yggdrasil. He landed alone.
In nine realms of wonder — on no single throne —
Not Asgard's great meadows, not Valhalla's bright mead,
Not even the stars bent themselves to his need.
Only the runes remained, burning his hands,
Eighteen secrets now carved into his bones like brands.

From nine vast realms, one wanderer came.
Not warrior. Not king. Not glory. Not fame.
Just a god and a tree and nine days of pain —
And a wisdom so sharp it will never be slain.

The Seeker's Final Verse
Where the 3 swords of the giants meet,
Count the realms that were hanging in fate's tangled thread,
Count the days that the Allfather hung nearly dead.
Nine for the realms. Nine for the days.
Now press them together through Yggdrasil's haze.
The mountain, the days, and the realms intertwined —
The answer was always what Odin resigned:
A place. Two numbers. No spaces between.
Speak it aloud — and unlock what it means.
